# Chronically Illing Out | E30 - Functioning With Compounding Conditions

## Guest Mack Devlin walks the panel through a stack of compounding diagnoses — muscular dystrophy, type 1 diabetes, AFib, chimerism — and the humor, faith, and creative work that keep him moving at 46.

> "That's just a benchmark. You know that does not determine the path of his life."
>
> _\~ The nurse at Children's Hospital Philadelphia, recalled by Mack Devlin \~_

### Chronic Illness In Review

This week's panel pulls a single thread — what does it actually take to function when your diagnoses keep stacking? Mack Devlin, a former journalist now medically retired, joins Nick, Beth, Soso, and Stephanie with one of the most compounding stacks the show has hosted: Becker's muscular dystrophy, rheumatoid arthritis, type 1 LADA diabetes, hypertension, depression, AFib, and chimerism — the rare condition where Mack absorbed his twin in utero and inherited a second set of genes. The conversation refuses to treat any of that as a tragedy montage. Instead it traces how someone twice told he should already be dead arrives at 46 with a non-profit in the works, a podcast, and a working impression of Kermit the Frog. It is the show's thesis distilled: compounding conditions don't add up to a sentence — they add up to a life that demands creative engineering.

The most concrete struggle in the conversation is Mack's recent fundraiser for insulin. After the company that makes Admelog was forced out of the U.S. market, his insurance refused to cover any of the alternatives, and the result was exactly what U.S. health policy critics have warned about for years — a chronic-illness patient running a public ask just to keep his pancreas-replacement therapy in the fridge. Beth, Soso, and Stephanie have all been on the receiving end of similar systems, with Beth living with cardiomyopathy, Soso navigating CPSD and fibromyalgia, and Stephanie hosting Freedom Over Fascism on adjacent ground. The episode does not dwell, but the implication lands: when a market-based drug supply fails, it rations by GoFundMe, and the people absorbing the cost are the ones who can least afford the cardiovascular tax of the stress.

Mack's most affecting story comes from age five at Children's Hospital Philadelphia. Awake in the middle of the night, he watched his mother tell a nurse, "I don't want my baby to die" — and heard the nurse answer, "That's just a benchmark. You know that does not determine the path of his life." Twelve came and went, then twenty-five came and went, and now Mack is 46 and crediting his mother for everything. The show keeps returning to this idea that medical timelines are predictions made on incomplete information, and the work of patients and the people who love them is to keep extending those predictions in directions doctors couldn't see. It isn't denial of prognosis — it's a refusal to confuse the prognosis with the person.

The middle stretch of the episode is, on its surface, off-topic — Lego flowers, karaoke, an impression of Grover, _There's a Monster at the End of This Book_ — and on closer listen, it is the topic. Soso names it directly: "When you're focusing on something like that, your brain can't focus on anxiety and pain." Mack's Joseph Pieper detour about the balance between leisure and work, and his own essay "Start With Bread," make the same case in different words: detachment from the tactile result of one's labor is a sickness all its own, and the antidote is to make small things you can hold. The Lego trellis, the karaoke night with Elijah and Fiona, the upcoming weekly readings of _The Jungle Book_ with his friend Teddy — these aren't hobbies pasted onto an illness narrative. They are the load-bearing structure that lets the illness narrative be something other than its own headline.

Three of the five panelists are simultaneously patient and caregiver. Beth is watching dementia work through her father, mother, and father-in-law. Soso has watched it move through five women on her father's side. Mack lost his mother to it and remembers her late-night Frasier monologues with the kind of humor only the bereaved earn. Nick lifts the conversation out of grief and into the next generation: his son has learned to call home for forgotten ADHD meds because he has internalized that the meds are tools, and Soso describes her daughter strategizing around her own dose based on the day's class schedule. The chronic-illness-and-dementia thread doesn't end on despair — it ends on kids who already know how to advocate for themselves, which is what generational work actually looks like.

Mack closes by previewing Bookbinders, the non-profit he is building to deliver books to disadvantaged children, plus _A Slice of a Life_, his deep-immersion podcast that sits with one person's history at a time. Beth previews her own pivot — after thirty years she is amicably ending her marriage and writing her way through it. Soso reminds the audience where to find Time For Life on Wednesdays, Stephanie points to 123 archived episodes of Freedom Over Fascism, and Nick recommits to building space for chronic-illness chats and intelligent-masculinity conversations even as the schedule keeps moving. Watching this panel hand the mic around is the simplest argument the show can make: compounding conditions do not exempt anyone from contribution — they sharpen the case for it.
