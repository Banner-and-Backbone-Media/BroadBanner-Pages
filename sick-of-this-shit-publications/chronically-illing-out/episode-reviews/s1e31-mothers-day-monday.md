# Chronically Illing Out | E31 - Mother's Day Monday

## Nick, Soso, and Stephanie process Mother's Day grief, sell-the-house transitions, and the politics of "earning" disability while Beth recovers and the panel previews Get Out the Guilt.

> "I mentioned it not to bring up sadness, but because for me it's important to acknowledge their passage in my life — be it short or be it long."
>
> _\~ Soso \~_

### Chronic Illness In Review

The Monday after Mother's Day arrives with a head cold, a missing co-host, and a transcript of grief most calendars refuse to make room for. Beth is out sick — appropriately, on a show about chronic illness — so Nick Paro, Soso, and Stephanie Wilson, PhD, hold the morning between them. What looks like a holiday postmortem becomes the show's quiet thesis: the chronically ill body keeps appointments with loss whether the dishes are done or not, and acknowledging that out loud is itself a discipline. Mother's Day is the wedge that pries the conversation open into surveillance capitalism, veteran disability politics, and a fledgling case for community-owned infrastructure — all of it tracked back, eventually, to who gets counted as fully human.

The episode's emotional center is Soso's account of her first daughter, born May 17, 2006, and lost May 29 of the same year. She names it not to mourn aloud but to refuse the cultural shorthand that treats short lives as unmentionable. That refusal sets the tone for the rest of the show. Stephanie names the parallel ache of Gen X women raised at the hinge of dramatic social change, who inherited mothers shaped by one world and built lives in another. Nick brings the third axis — fatherhood-adjacent gratitude, complicated by his own underweather Mother's Day weekend. The panel doesn't resolve any of this. It models the harder move: holding good and bad simultaneously, which is exactly the daily labor of chronic illness.

From there the conversation pivots into transition logistics, which on this show is the same conversation in different clothes. Soso has sold her Quebec house in ten days. Stephanie is staging a Massachusetts house for a June listing while shepherding her son into a counselor job and college. Nick is laying water and power lines for what is, technically, a two-home family compound — a phrase he immediately disclaims with the day's funniest extended bit about Ramtha cult machine-gun turrets and the gulf between his actual life and billionaire infrastructure. The joke lands because it carries the argument: the right to build accessible space on your own terms is its own form of resistance, and you don't need an island to do it.

That argument deepens when Nick — drawing on his time on the Alexa healthcare team that pushed Amazon toward HIPAA and GDPR compliance — pivots to data sovereignty. The accessibility features built into household tech are the same features that harvest health data, and the disability community has not historically been able to speak with one voice about the trade. Citing Nieta Greene, Nick walks through the wedge: veteran disability culture inherits the merit logic of combat — patches, units, the toxic competition for "real" disability — and that logic bleeds into civilian disability spaces. Soso pushes back hard against "earning" a disability, with her cousin's quadriplegia and her own CPTSD as evidence. Nick agrees, then sharpens the point: the only way out is solidarity strong enough to say *no, you can't touch our fucking data* — and mean it across veteran and civilian lines.

The closing stretch threads the civic work back through the personal. Nick previews afternoon interviews with Salam Bhatti (Virginia CD5) and Eric Turwey (Oklahoma CD2), the latter notable for foregrounding treaty law and Cherokee Nation representation — a frame that pairs cleanly with the episode's argument about under-represented voices having to assemble themselves into one. Soso reminds Nick to rest, calls out Courtney for doing the same back to him, and books a Time for Karaoke special on Thursday evening for her birthday. Beth's pre-recorded Reset Refresh meditation gives the show its final breath, and Nick titles next week on air: *Get Out the Guilt*.

What lingers is the show doing in miniature what it argues for at scale. A panel down a member doesn't cancel; it adjusts. Grief gets named without being staged. Tech is neither demonized nor swallowed whole — it gets interrogated for what it costs the disability community in data, and what it returns in accessibility. And the Mother's Day frame, which began as small talk about brunch, ends up doing the heavy lifting it always does: reminding listeners that whoever you are this morning — celebrating, mourning, both — there is a seat at this table, and the price of admission is just being honest about it.
